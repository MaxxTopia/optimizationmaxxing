# The grind layer — sleep, sessions, warmups, body

Asta Mode handles the rig. This handles the human running it.

No amount of -3 ms helps if your aim falls off after match 4. The pros
people quote on settings are *also* the pros most rigorous about sleep,
session structure, and warmup routines. That part rarely makes it into
config screenshots. Here's the cited version.

## Sleep — the most-undervalued performance lever

Aim consistency drops measurably after sub-7-hour sleep nights. The
Stanford Sleep + Athletic Performance series (Cheri Mah's research with
the Stanford basketball team) showed: **extending sleep from 7 → 10 hours
improved free-throw accuracy 9.0% and 3-point accuracy 9.2%.** Reaction
time improved 5.4%. That's measured in basketball but the mechanism is
the same — fine motor + decision speed both decay under sleep debt.

For Fortnite specifically, Bugha's published training notes treat sleep as
a *training tool*, not a recovery activity. The implication: night-before
sleep determines tomorrow's ceiling, not the other way around.

Practical:

- **8–9 hours minimum** the night before any tournament.
- **Same time every night.** Circadian consistency moves the bar more
  than total hours by themselves.
- **No screen ~30 minutes before bed.** Blue light delays melatonin
  release by 30–45 minutes. Yes, this includes Fortnite VOD review.
- **No caffeine after 2 PM** (8-hour half-life on most adult metabolisms).

## Session structure — 90 / 10 over flat hours

Pro session cadence isn't "play until you stop wanting to." It's
structured:

- **90-minute focus blocks**, then a 10-minute walk + hydration break.
  Cognitive performance research (Anders Ericsson's deliberate-practice
  body of work) shows performance drops measurably after ~90 minutes of
  focused work without rest.
- **3–4 blocks per training day max.** That's 4.5–6 hours of *focused*
  work — different from 8 hours of background-scrolling-while-grinding.
- **Different drill per block.** Block 1 aim warmup, block 2 1v1s,
  block 3 scrim, block 4 VOD review. Cycling drill type prevents
  motor pattern locking + keeps the work novel.

Aussie Antics talks about this from a personal-training background. He
pre-Fortnite: trained athletes professionally. The framework he applies
to streaming + content + grinding is the same one you'd apply to lifting
or running — periodization beats grinding-til-empty every time.

## Warmup — fixed routine, not improvised

Pros run identical warmups every session. Reasons:

1. **Calibration.** Same drills daily = same baseline = you can detect
   when your aim is "off today" before it costs you a tournament point.
2. **Routine = lower anxiety.** Game-day jitters compound; a warmup you
   do without thinking shrinks them.
3. **Mechanical activation.** Cold mouse hand misses easy flicks. Warm
   hand doesn't. Takes ~5–10 minutes of low-stakes drilling to get there.

A representative pro warmup (from Noahreyli's published Fortnite-Masterclass
routine — he plays + coaches at the highest level):

```
1. Aim Botz (CS2 / Aimlab equivalent) — 5 min, free flicks
2. KovaaK 1wall6targets TE — 5 min, micro-correction work
3. KovaaK Smoothbot or similar tracking — 5 min, smooth pursuit
4. Edit course — 3 min, mechanical activation for builds
5. Real 1v1 — 5 min, integration into game state
```

Total: ~25 minutes. Don't skip it for ranked. Don't extend it past 40 min
either — past that, you're spending shots that should be going into the
match.

Tools:

- **KovaaK FPS Aim Trainer** ($10, Steam) — the de facto standard.
- **AimLab** (free) — easier UI, smaller community library.
- **Fortnite creative aim courses** — search "1v1 aim course" in
  Discover for community-tested ones. Vinny1x and Pandvil run the
  best-known.

## Body — wrist, posture, hydration

Aussie Antics' personal-training background shows up here. The body cost
of a 6-hour grinding session is real and accumulates.

- **Mouse wrist.** Floating-arm aim (low sens, full forearm motion)
  protects the wrist long-term. Wrist-only aim (high sens) wears the
  ulnar nerve faster — see "carpal tunnel" complaints in old-pro retire-
  ment posts. If you're high sens and your wrist tingles, lower your
  sens before it becomes a chronic problem.
- **Posture.** Monitor at eye level. Chair tall enough that your knees
  bend at 90°+. Keyboard low enough that your elbows hit ~95°. Long-
  session pros all run the same setup not because they all read the
  same article — because chair geometry that's wrong for 6 hours is
  measurably painful.
- **Hydration.** Cognitive performance drops 5–10% at 2% dehydration.
  That's about 1–2 hours of focused work without water. Keep a bottle
  on the desk.
- **Eye strain.** 20-20-20 rule: every 20 minutes, look at something
  20 feet away for 20 seconds. Built-in to the 90/10 break cadence
  above — if you walk away from the monitor, you've hit it.

## VOD review — don't skip the boring part

Most ranked pros watch their own VODs more than their opponents'.
Three questions every endgame loss should answer:

1. **Where did I die?** Position on map, height advantage, tile.
2. **What was the trigger?** Shot taken without info, cone-build into
   open angle, heal mid-fight.
3. **What was the actually-correct play given the info I had?** Not
   the play with hindsight. The one with what you saw at decision time.

15 minutes per session, post-match. Not exciting. Compounds enormously.

## Why this matters more than most people admit

Software tuning gives you 70% of the rig gap. The grind layer gives you
the *consistency* to actually use those 70%. A 25 ms latency budget on a
sleep-deprived day is no better than a 75 ms budget on a fresh day —
your reaction time + decision-making degrades faster than your latency
gain compensates.

The kids who actually catch the pros aren't doing one of these well.
They're doing all of them, repetitively, when no one's watching.

That's the version of you that doesn't quit. Build it.
